Buffett's Intrinsic Value Formula

Buffett's Intrinsic Value Formula is a valuation method used to estimate the true worth of a company by analyzing its earnings, expected growth rate, and required rate of return. This formula is grounded in fundamental analysis, focusing on the long-term earning power of a company rather than short-term market fluctuations. It is a quick proxy to help investors determine whether a stock is undervalued or overvalued relative to its intrinsic value.

When to use Buffett's Intrinsic Value Formula

This formula is particularly useful for value investors who prioritize long-term investments in companies with strong fundamentals. It is most effective when evaluating established companies with a consistent track record of earnings and growth. This can be a valuable proxy to identify to identify stocks potentially trading below their intrinsic value, and which might warrant a deeper dive.

Cautions and Limitations

While Buffett's Intrinsic Value Formula is a powerful tool for assessing a company’s value, it’s important to understand its role as a preliminary or proxy method before diving into a more thorough analysis:

  • Reliance on Earnings and Growth Estimates: The formula depends heavily on accurate estimates of future earnings and growth rates, which can be difficult to predict. As such, it should be viewed as a rough estimate rather than a definitive valuation, particularly for companies in volatile industries or those with unpredictable earnings patterns.
  • Assumption of Constant Growth Rate: This formula assumes a steady and constant growth rate over time, which may not align with the realities of fluctuating market conditions, competitive pressures, or changes in the broader business environment. As a result, it provides a simplified view that should be validated with more detailed analysis.
  • Exclusion of Qualitative Factors: The formula focuses primarily on quantitative data, overlooking qualitative aspects such as a company’s competitive advantages, management quality, or industry trends. These factors can play a significant role in determining a company’s true value and should be explored in subsequent, more comprehensive evaluations.

Definitions

  • Trailing Earnings Per Share (EPS): Enter the company's earnings per share for the most recent twelve-month period.
  • Expected EPS Growth: Enter the expected long-term growth rate for the company's earnings per share. This should be a reasonable estimate and should not be too high as it would result in overvaluation of the company. A typical range is between 5% to 15%.
  • 10-Year Treasury Rate: Enter the yield of the 10-year Treasury bond. This is a risk-free rate and is used as a proxy for the rate of return an investor could earn without taking any risk.
  • Expected Market Return: Enter the expected long-term return for the stock market as a whole. This should reflect the historical average return of the stock market and should be adjusted for any expected changes in the future. A typical range is between 6% to 8%.
  • Stock Beta: Enter the beta of the company's stock, which measures the level of risk associated with the stock compared to the overall market.
